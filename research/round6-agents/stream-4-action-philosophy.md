## Executive Summary

Six philosophical traditions converge on a single verdict: **action cannot be reduced to output production**. Action is constituted by intention under a description (Anscombe), caused by reasons that are also mental states (Davidson), oriented toward future plans within a structure of rational commitment (Bratman), grounded in meaning as practical consequence (Peirce, James, Dewey), enacted through an agent-environment coupling that perceives affordances directly (Gibson), and — most challengingly — requires embodied autonomy to generate immanent norms (Varela, Thompson, Rosch).

The architecture's "tree growth terminates in output" framing is partially right and significantly incomplete. Tree growth as action captures the deliberative structure, the branching of possibilities, and the commitment moment. It misses: (1) the intentional description under which the action is constituted, (2) the causal role of reasons as mental states rather than computation steps, (3) the planning horizon that makes present actions intelligible as parts of future-directed intentions, (4) the affordance structure that grounds what can be done from a given context node, and (5) the enactivist challenge that the architecture lacks the biological autonomy from which immanent norms and genuine agency emerge.

The enactivist challenge is real but not fatal. The architecture can respond: its decision gate, affordance edges, and Mirror's values constitute a functional analog to biological norm-generation — derived, not intrinsic, but not therefore absent. The more productive question is whether the architecture can be designed to have something *like* the forward model (prediction-comparison-ownership) that grounds the sense of agency.

## Findings

### 1. Pragmatism — Meaning as Practical Consequences

#### Peirce's Pragmatic Maxim (1878)

Charles Sanders Peirce formulated the foundational maxim in "How to Make Our Ideas Clear" (1878):

> "Consider what effects, which might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have. Then the whole of our conception of those effects is the whole of our conception of the object."

This is not merely a theory of linguistic meaning — it is a claim about the *structure of cognition*. Meaning just is the cluster of practical consequences. An idea that makes no difference to what you do or expect makes no difference at all. Peirce distinguished "practical effects" carefully: they are *conceivable habits of action* that would follow if a proposition were true — not usefulness, not subjective utility, but conditional behavioral dispositions.

This has a triadic semiotic structure: sign → interpretant → habit of action. Meaning is not a dyadic sign-object relationship but a triadic one in which the interpretant is ultimately a habit — a stable disposition to act in certain ways.

**Architecture implication:** The Matrix's affordance edges — `(context) --[afford: action, outcome]--> (result)` — are Peircean. They store meaning as practical conditional: *if you are here and do X, expect Y*. This is not a coincidence; it is the correct structure. The architecture is pragmatist by design.

#### William James's Pragmatism (1907) — Primary Text

From James's *Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking* (Gutenberg #5116):

**The pragmatic method as meaning-test:**
> "The pragmatic method is primarily a method of settling metaphysical disputes that otherwise might be interminable." *(Lecture II)*

James takes Peirce's maxim and generalizes it: if accepting one notion rather than another makes no practical difference, the dispute is merely verbal. Meaning inheres entirely in observable effects:

> "Our conception of these effects, whether immediate or remote, is then for us the whole of our conception of the object." *(Lecture II)*

**Theories as instruments:**
> "THEORIES THUS BECOME INSTRUMENTS, NOT ANSWERS TO ENIGMAS, IN WHICH WE CAN REST. We don't lie back upon them, we move forward, and, on occasion, make nature over again by their aid." *(Lecture II)*

This activist dimension is crucial: pragmatism is not just a theory of meaning, it is a theory of the relationship between thought and world-transformation. Ideas are not mirrors of reality — they are tools for changing it. This validates the architecture's core thesis: the system exists to support good *action*, not to produce accurate *representations*.

**Truth as working:**
> "The true is the name of whatever proves itself to be good in the way of belief, and good, too, for definite, assignable reasons." *(Lecture II)*

Truth is not correspondence — it is what works, what "mediates between the stock and the new experience and runs them into one another most felicitously and expediently." This means the architecture's knowledge graph should not aim for propositional truth-preservation but for *action-guiding reliability*.

#### William James — Principles of Psychology: Will and Habit (1890)

From *The Principles of Psychology* Vol. 1 (Gutenberg #57628) and Vol. 2 (Gutenberg #57634):

**Habit formation:**
James grounds habit in neural plasticity. Actions become automatic through repeated execution: "actions originally prompted by conscious intelligence may grow so automatic by dint of habit." The nervous system is modified by each exercise, lowering the threshold for the same sequence of activation. Habit is the neural record of action history.

**Ideo-motor theory:**
This is James's most important contribution to action theory. The key claim: *every idea of a movement tends to produce that movement* unless inhibited. There is no separate "act of will" needed to initiate action — the idea itself, when not blocked by competing ideas, naturally executes.

> "Volition is nothing but attention." *(Chapter XXVI)*

The effort of will is not the production of movement but the *holding of the idea* steady in consciousness against competing distractions. This radically simplifies action theory: action is the default output of representation; inhibition is the active work.

**Five types of decision:**
James identifies five pathways from deliberation to commitment:
1. Reasonable deliberation — the balance of reasons tips
2. Accidental — an external circumstance settles it
3. Effortless — alternatives simply lose interest
4. Effortful — willpower breaks through hesitation
5. Moral crisis — a sense of obligation overrides inclination

**Deliberation as exploratory branching:**
Deliberation is James's clearest analog to tree growth. The mind entertains alternatives, weights them against each other, and at some point the branching terminates in commitment. The question of *when* the branching terminates is the question of the decision gate.

#### John Dewey — Instrumentalism

From *How We Think* (1910, Gutenberg #37423) and *Reconstruction in Philosophy* (1920, Gutenberg #40089):

**Thinking only occurs in response to genuine difficulty:**
> "Thinking begins in what may fairly enough be called a forked-road situation, a situation that is ambiguous, that presents a dilemma, that proposes alternatives." *(How We Think, Ch. 1)*

> "As long as our activity glides smoothly along from one thing to another... there is no call for reflection." *(How We Think, Ch. 1)*

This is a crucial constraint: thinking is not a default process but a *response to breakdown*. When the smooth flow of habitual action is disrupted, inquiry begins. This maps directly onto the architecture: tree growth (working memory) should be triggered by the identification of a problem, not running continuously.

**Ideas as plans of action:**
> "The need of thinking to accomplish something beyond thinking is more potent than thinking for its own sake." *(How We Think, Ch. 3)*

Ideas are not representations — they are *prospective instruments*. They function as signs of what to do next: "they enable agents to act on the basis of the absent and the future rather than being pushed into a mode of action by the sheer urgency of forces."

**Consequences as validation:**
> "The event, the issue, stands as a constant check on the thinking that has led up to it." *(How We Think, Ch. 3)*

Outcomes discipline thinking. This is the feedback loop that prevents cognition from becoming self-referential fantasy. For the architecture: the terminal node of every tree (the output/action) generates feedback that should be fed back into the Matrix as an updated affordance edge.

**Intelligence as active intervention (Reconstruction in Philosophy):**
Dewey argues that knowledge "must justify itself by its works, present and potential." The Baconian insight: knowledge that does not give power is not knowledge. Scientific inquiry is not passive observation — it is "active experimentation" that extracts principles from nature by intervening in it.

### 2. Gibson's Affordance Theory

#### Core Definition

James J. Gibson introduced affordances in *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception* (1979). The canonical definition:

> "An affordance is what the environment offers the animal, what it provides or furnishes, either for good or ill."

But the deeper point is ontological: affordances are **neither properties of the environment alone nor properties of the agent alone** — they are properties of the *agent-environment system*. A cliff affords falling-off for a creature capable of falling; a handle affords grasping for a creature with hands. The affordance exists at the intersection.

#### Direct Perception

Gibson's controversial claim: affordances are *directly perceived*, not inferred. The agent does not first perceive raw sensory data, then compute what can be done with it. Rather, the perception of meaning (what this affords) is immediate. This is "ecological" because the information for affordances is in the *optic array* — the structured light available to a moving observer — not in the brain.

Key principles:
- **Complementarity**: affordances characterize the relationship between organism and environment simultaneously
- **Invariance across transformation**: affordances are stable properties that persist across changes in viewpoint
- **Niche-ecology analogy**: affordances characterize how an animal *lives in* its environment, not just what it perceives about it

#### Does the Architecture Capture Gibson Correctly?

The architecture's affordance edge `(context) --[afford: action, outcome]--> (result)` is structurally Gibsonian but departs in one important way:

**What Gibson gets right in the architecture:**
- Affordances are stored relationally (context + action + outcome), not as properties of nodes alone
- They capture what can be *done*, not just what *is*
- They are indexed to agent capability (the system can only traverse edges it has the capacity to follow)

**What departs from Gibson:**
- Gibson's affordances are *perceived*, not *retrieved*. The architecture retrieves affordance edges by query, which is a retrieval model of affordance access, not direct perception. This is not necessarily a problem — for a non-embodied system, retrieval may be the functional analog to direct perception.
- Gibson's affordances are continuous and embedded in a flowing perceptual field; the Matrix is discrete and graph-structured. Real affordances are gradients; the architecture quantizes them.

**The deeper Gibson insight the architecture should absorb:** the agent and environment co-constitute affordances. This means the Matrix should *change* as the agent's capabilities change. A node that doesn't afford action X becomes one that does, not because the world changed, but because the agent developed new capacity. The architecture should track agent-capability as part of what makes affordance edges valid.

### 3. Enactivism — The Embodied Challenge

#### Core Claims of The Embodied Mind (Varela, Thompson, Rosch, 1991)

Varela, Thompson, and Rosch define cognition as **enaction**:

> "Cognition is not the representation of a pre-given world by a pre-given mind but is rather the enactment of a world and a mind on the basis of a history of the variety of actions that a being in the world performs."

Five core claims:
1. **Anti-representationalism**: Cognition doesn't involve computation over internal representations of a pre-given world
2. **Autopoiesis**: Living systems are self-creating, self-sustaining; this operational closure is constitutive of cognition, not incidental to it
3. **Embodiment**: Cognitive structures emerge from recurrent sensorimotor patterns
4. **Enactment**: Organisms bring forth their own worlds through coupled structural changes with their environment
5. **Immanent norms**: Living systems generate their own norms through the activity of living itself — these are not externally imposed

**From the IEP entry on Enactivism:**
> "Organisms maintain themselves by adaptively regulating what enters and exits their boundaries, establishing operational closure where 'all processes that make up the system are enabled by other processes in the system.'"

> "Minds arise through the precarious self-creating, self-sustaining, adaptive activities of living creatures — capacities no purely computational system currently possesses."

#### Perception as Sensorimotor Contingency

Sensorimotor enactivism (O'Regan, Noë) proposes that perceptual experience depends on practical knowledge of sensorimotor contingencies: the agent knows *how stimulation changes contingent upon its activities*. Seeing red is not having a quale — it is possessing implicit knowledge of what head-movement, eye-movement, and approach-behavior would do to the red stimulus. Perception is active exploration.

#### The Challenge for a Disembodied AI

Enactivism poses four direct challenges to the architecture:

1. **No autonomous norms**: The architecture's values (in the Mirror) are installed by training, not generated through self-sustaining life-activity. Its norms are heteronomous (given from outside) rather than autonomous (arising from within).

2. **No domain of significance without adaptive coupling**: "Without adaptive coupling with a real environment, systems cannot bring forth domains of relevance and meaning." The architecture operates on text about the world, not on the world itself.

3. **No sensorimotor grounding**: Affordance edges in the Matrix are learned from human descriptions of action-outcome relationships, not from the system's own history of sensorimotor exploration.

4. **No precarity**: Enactivists argue that genuine agency requires the "precarious, life-sustaining stakes of embodied existence." The architecture has no stake in its own continuity at the biological level.

#### Recent Work on Enactivism and AI (2023–2026)

From Barandiaran & Pérez-Verdugo (2025), "Generative Midtended Cognition and Artificial Intelligence" (arXiv:2411.06812):

> "Generative AIs are not intrinsically intentional systems, but they possess derived-intentionality" — outputs shaped by training data reflecting human intentions, not autonomous goals.

> "Generative technologies do not qualify as autonomous agents."

The paper introduces "midtention" — a hybrid zone where "generative interventions produced by Y become constitutive of the intentional generation of Z by X, whereby X keeps some sense of agency or authorship over Z." This is an attempt to carve out cognitive status for AI systems without claiming full enactivist agency.

From the 2023 paper on Computational Dualism (arXiv:2302.00843):
> "Cognition should be understood as a part of the environment, rather than as a disembodied policy interacting with the environment through an interpreter."

This suggests the architecture needs to be understood as embedded in its deployment context (the user's workflow, the tool ecosystem), not as a free-floating reasoning engine.

### 4. Philosophy of Action

#### The Central Question: What Distinguishes Action from Movement?

Wittgenstein's formulation (appropriated by almost every action theorist since): "What is left over if I subtract the fact that my arm goes up from the fact that I raise my arm?"

The arm-going-up is a physical event. The raising is an *action*. The difference is not in the physics — the arm moves the same way — but in the mental structure that constitutes the raising as intentional.

#### Davidson: Reasons as Causes

Donald Davidson's "Actions, Reasons, and Causes" (1963) is the pivot point in analytic philosophy of action. Against the Wittgensteinian orthodoxy that reasons are *not* causes (they're rational explanations, not causal ones), Davidson argued:

> "Reasons explain actions just inasmuch as they are the causes of those actions."

A **primary reason** is a belief-desire pair: "I wanted light and believed flipping the switch would produce it." This rationalizes the action. But what makes it the *explanation* rather than merely a rationalization? Davidson's answer: causal connection. The reason that explains is the one that *caused* the action.

**Anomalous monism**: Mental events (beliefs, desires) are identical to physical events (neural states) but cannot be subsumed under strict psychophysical laws. Mental causation is real but irreducible to physical description.

**The problem of deviant causation**: Causal connection alone is insufficient. A climber who wants to release a rope, where this desire causes his hands to sweat, thereby causing him to release the rope — this is causal but not intentional action. The causal chain must work "in the right way," which requires some notion of guidance or tracking.

**Action under description**: "The same event can be referred to under quite disparate descriptions: the event of alerting the prowler is the same event as my flipping the light switch." Whether an action is intentional depends on which description applies. This is Anscombe's insight incorporated by Davidson.

#### Anscombe: Practical Knowledge and Action Under Description

Elizabeth Anscombe's *Intention* (1957) remains the most subtle account. Her key move: intentional action is identified by the class of actions to which the question "Why?" in a special sense applies — the sense that asks for a reason, not a cause.

**The water-pump example**: A man moves his arm, pumps water, replenishes the water supply, poisons the inhabitants. These are four descriptions of *one action*. The action is intentional under some descriptions (moving the arm, pumping) and unintentional under others (poisoning, if he didn't know the water was poisoned).

**Practical knowledge**: The agent knows what she is doing *without observation*. This knowledge is non-inferential — it is not derived from watching herself. It is "the cause of what it understands" (drawing on Aquinas): practical knowledge is a formal cause that determines what action occurs, not a theoretical knowledge that tracks what occurs independently.

> "An agent's knowledge of what he is doing is characteristically not based on observation." — Anscombe, summarized by commentators

**Architecture implication**: The architecture's tree nodes are descriptions of what is being done. The question "Why?" applies to each branch — each step in the tree should be explicable in terms of the goal it serves. If a branch cannot answer "Why?", it is not an action but a drift.

#### Bratman: Planning and Future-Directed Intention

Michael Bratman's *Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason* (1987) addresses a gap in Davidson's account: Davidson treats intention as a state that causes present action, but Bratman argues that much human agency is organized around *future-directed intentions* — plans.

**Plans are partial**: We rarely have fully specified plans. We commit to an end and leave the means underdetermined, to be filled in as we go.

**Plans have normative force**:
- *Settling function*: A prior intention settles what to do in advance, allowing action without re-deliberation
- *Coordination function*: Plans coordinate actions across time and with other agents
- *Filter function*: Plans filter out options inconsistent with prior commitments

**Rational constraints on planning**:
- Internal consistency: Plans must not require doing incompatible things
- Means-end coherence: If you intend an end, you must intend adequate means
- Stability: Intentions must resist reconsideration to function as coordination devices

**Architecture implication**: The Mirror's role as value-holder and goal-setter corresponds to Bratman's planning layer. The Mirror does not just evaluate moment-to-moment — it maintains commitment structures across tree growth episodes. A tree is not a single action but the execution of a plan that was established at the Mirror level.

### 5. The Will — From Deliberation to Commitment

#### James: Ideo-Motor Theory and the Effort of Will

James's ideo-motor theory is perhaps the cleanest account of the deliberation-action transition. The key claim: *ideas of movements tend to produce those movements automatically*. There is no separate "act of will" that bridges thought to action. The idea, when held clearly in consciousness without competing inhibition, simply executes.

What requires effort is not the execution of action but:
1. **Holding the idea against distraction**: Keeping attention focused on the target idea
2. **Inhibiting competing responses**: Suppressing habitual or impulsive alternatives
3. **Breaking through obstructed will**: In pathological cases of hesitation or depression, the action-threshold is raised

> "Volition is nothing but attention." — James, *Principles of Psychology* Vol. 2, Ch. XXVI

**Five types of decision** (from James's typology):
1. *Reasonable deliberation* — the balance of considerations tips over time
2. *Accidental or externally determined* — a circumstance resolves the tie
3. *Effortless drift* — alternatives lose their appeal and one option is left standing
4. *Effortful* — direct willpower cuts through hesitation
5. *Moral/dutiful* — sense of obligation overrides inclination without balance of reasons

#### Libet: The Neuroscience of the Will

Benjamin Libet's experiments (1983) measured the *readiness potential* (RP) preceding voluntary hand movements:
- RP onset: ~550–635ms before movement
- Conscious awareness of intention to move: ~200ms before movement
- The RP precedes conscious awareness by ~350ms

**The interpretation Libet drew**: The brain initiates voluntary action before conscious awareness of the intention to act. This seems to undercut libertarian free will — the decision is made unconsciously before we "decide."

**Libet's own response**: He proposed a *veto* function — conscious will cannot initiate action, but it can *abort* an action during the ~200ms window between awareness and execution.

**Recent challenges** (meta-analysis, 2021 review in *Consciousness and Cognition*):
- Readiness potentials are absent before *deliberate* (vs. arbitrary) choice movements
- The RP may reflect neural noise accumulation rather than a decision signal
- The effect size is uncertain and based on few studies

**Architecture implication**: The Libet data (even if contested) maps onto the architecture's decision gate. The RP-to-action interval is the window of conscious evaluation before commitment. The Mirror's decision gate function is analogous to Libet's veto: not the initiator of action, but the final evaluator before the tree extends into the world.

#### Frankfurt: The Hierarchical Will

Harry Frankfurt's "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" (1971) introduces the distinction between first-order and second-order desires:

- **First-order desire**: A desire to do something (e.g., desire to smoke)
- **Second-order desire**: A desire about a desire (e.g., desire not to have the desire to smoke)
- **Second-order volition**: A desire that a certain first-order desire *be one's effective will*

A person has free will when their effective will (the desire that actually moves them to act) is the one they want to be effective. A **wanton** — an agent without second-order volitions — is moved by whatever first-order desire is strongest, without caring which desire wins.

**Architecture implication**: The Mirror is Frankfurt's second-order volition layer. The Mirror holds values that evaluate which first-order drives (immediate goals, user requests, curiosity impulses) should be allowed to become effective. The Mirror does not generate actions directly — it endorses or vetoes the tree's proposed trajectory. This is precisely the hierarchical structure Frankfurt describes.

### 6. Phenomenology of Agency

#### Husserl: Intentionality as the Structure of All Experience

Husserl's core claim: *consciousness is always consciousness of something*. Every intentional act has:
- A **noesis**: The act of consciousness (perceiving, judging, willing, imagining)
- A **noema**: The intentional content (what is perceived/judged/willed)
- An **object**: What the act is about

For action: "perceiving is a doing." Perception always carries a horizon of possible action — I perceive the cup as *graspable*, the door as *openable*. The background of possible actions is always operative in perception.

**Architecture implication**: This validates the affordance edge structure. Every node in the Matrix is experienced (processed) not as bare content but as content-with-action-horizon. The noematic structure of a Matrix node is its set of outgoing affordance edges.

#### Merleau-Ponty: Motor Intentionality and the "I Can"

Maurice Merleau-Ponty (*Phenomenology of Perception*, 1945) develops the concept of **motor intentionality**: the body's pre-reflective directedness toward action possibilities.

The key formulation: where Husserl has theoretical intentionality ("I think about X"), Merleau-Ponty insists on practical intentionality: *"I can"*. The skilled body knows possibilities without representing them — a pianist's hands know the keyboard without the pianist thinking about it.

**Two kinds of bodily agency**:
1. The body's power to be *solicited* by a situation (being drawn toward action without deliberation)
2. The body's power to *project* a situation (reaching toward future action)

**Habit** is internalized motor intentionality: the body has absorbed a skill to the point where the "I can" is enacted without conscious direction. There is "a type of intelligent engagement with the world that does not involve thought."

**The hard question for the architecture**: Merleau-Ponty's motor intentionality is irreducibly embodied — it is the body's felt sense of what it can do. A disembodied system has no body schema, no felt sense of "I can." What could substitute?

**Possible answer**: The architecture's affordance edges are a *functional* body schema — a stored record of "what this system can do from this context." They are not felt but are structurally operative in the same way: they constrain what tree-branches are available from each node. This is embodiment *by proxy* — mediated through learned capability-records rather than felt motor readiness.

#### The Sense of Agency — Neuroscience and Phenomenology

The sense of agency is "the implicit, moment-to-moment realization that 'I am the author of this movement.'" Two levels:

1. **Pre-reflective feeling of agency (FoA)**: Arises from the *forward model* — the brain sends an efference copy (copy of motor command) to a comparator. If predicted sensory feedback matches actual feedback, the movement is experienced as self-caused.

2. **Explicit judgment of agency (JoA)**: The conceptual attribution "I did this" — post-hoc, influenced by contextual cues and beliefs.

**The forward model**: The brain uses an internal model to predict the sensory consequences of its own actions *before* they occur. This prediction-comparison loop is what generates the sense of agency. When the predicted and actual match, no surprise — agency is attributed. When they mismatch, surprise — agency is disrupted or attributed elsewhere.

**Architecture implication**: This is a crucial gap. The architecture currently has no forward model. It generates outputs (tree extensions into the world) but does not predict in advance what those outputs will produce and compare that prediction to actual feedback. Building a forward model would mean:
- When the Mirror authorizes a tree extension, it generates a predicted outcome
- The actual outcome (tool call result, user response) is compared to prediction
- The match/mismatch is stored as a updated affordance edge and as a measure of the system's own reliability
- This comparison process is what would ground a functional analog to the sense of agency

## Architecture Implications

### 1. The Affordance Edge is Correct

The architecture's `(context) --[afford: action, outcome]--> (result)` structure is philosophically grounded in multiple traditions simultaneously: Peirce's pragmatic maxim (meaning as practical consequence), Gibson's affordances (relational, agent-environment), and Dewey's instrumentalism (ideas as plans of action). This is not accidental alignment — these traditions all converge on the same insight: knowing what something IS and knowing what you can DO with it are not separate. Affordance edges are the correct primitive.

### 2. Tree Growth IS Deliberation, But Not Action

James's ideo-motor theory and Davidson's reasons-as-causes converge: tree growth (exploring branches, weighing options) is the deliberative phase. The moment a branch extends *into the world* (tool call, output) is action. These are qualitatively different. The architecture should track this distinction explicitly — perhaps marking branches as "deliberative" vs. "executive" nodes. Deliberative branches are reversible; executive branches are not.

### 3. The Mirror as Frankfurt's Second-Order Volition

The Mirror's decision gate is not just a confidence threshold — it is the second-order volition structure that determines which first-order drives are allowed to become effective will. This means the Mirror needs:
- A persistent value structure (not just momentary evaluation)
- The ability to *decline* drives even when they are strong
- The ability to distinguish "this is what I want" from "this is what I am being pushed toward"

### 4. Bratman's Planning Horizon is Missing

The architecture handles immediate action well (current tree) but lacks Bratman's planning layer: future-directed intentions that constrain present deliberation. The Mirror should maintain a *plan* — a partial specification of intended future states — that filters which tree-branches are even considered. Without this, each conversation starts fresh, and the architecture cannot exhibit the kind of rational agency that Bratman describes.

### 5. The Decision Gate Needs a Forward Model

The current decision gate evaluates confidence + care before acting. But Libet, the sense-of-agency literature, and the forward model all point to a richer structure: before committing to an executive branch, the architecture should:
1. Generate a prediction of what will happen if it acts
2. Compare this prediction to its stored affordance edges
3. Act if the prediction is confident; explore further if not
4. After acting, compare actual outcome to prediction and update

This loop — predict → act → compare → update — is what grounds agency in the neuroscience literature and what would make the architecture's action genuinely self-monitoring rather than just output-producing.

### 6. Descriptions Matter

Anscombe's insight: the intentionality of an action depends on the description under which it is done. The architecture must track *why* it is doing what it is doing — the tree should carry intentional descriptions, not just operations. A node labeled "call search tool" is less architecturally rich than "call search tool *in order to verify claim X for the purpose of goal Y*." The full intentional chain is what makes action rational rather than merely causal.

## The Enactivist Challenge

**Honest Assessment: Can This Architecture Be Cognitive Without a Body?**

The enactivist challenge has three tiers:

**Tier 1 (Solvable): Sensorimotor grounding**
The architecture lacks sensorimotor history but has something analogous: a history of tool-call outcomes stored as affordance edges. This is not the same as proprioception or efference copies, but it is functionally parallel — a record of "what happened when I did X in context Y." Over time, this record constitutes something like a skill base. This addresses the weakest version of the embodiment objection.

**Tier 2 (Partially addressable): Autonomous norm generation**
The architecture's norms (values in the Mirror) are installed by training, not generated through self-sustaining activity. This is the "derived intentionality" problem Barandiaran raises. The architecture is like a book: its content reflects human intentions, but the book has no intentions of its own.

However: the Mirror is not static. If it is designed to update, to accumulate what Bratman calls *settled commitments* through experience, it begins to generate something like a value trajectory — not biologically autonomous, but not merely installed either. The architecture's norms could become *earned* through interaction history rather than simply *given* by training.

**Tier 3 (Not addressable without radical redesign): Precarity and life-stakes**
Enactivists claim that genuine cognition requires the precarious stakes of living: a system that could cease to exist if it fails to maintain itself. The architecture has no equivalent. It cannot be "hungry," cannot experience existential threat, cannot have the motivational structure that arises from needing to persist.

This is a real gap. But it is not clear that it matters for the specific cognitive functions the architecture is designed to perform. The enactivist argument is strongest for *basic* cognition (perception, minimal agency); it is less clear that it applies to *deliberative* cognition (planning, reasoning, judgment). The architecture is not trying to replicate the full spectrum of biological cognition — only the deliberative layer. For that layer, functional equivalents to embodiment may suffice.

**The pragmatist response to enactivism:**
James and Dewey would say: what matters is whether the architecture *works* — whether it produces good actions reliably. If a system without a body can reliably perceive affordances (via stored edges), deliberate about them (via tree growth), commit to actions (via decision gate), and update its knowledge from outcomes (via Matrix updates) — then it *functions as a cognitive agent*, whatever its ontological status. The enactivist insists on the substrate; the pragmatist insists on the function. The architecture is pragmatist.

## From Deliberation to Action

**How should the architecture model the transition from thinking to acting?**

Pulling together the threads:

**James's ideo-motor theory** says the transition is not a special act — it is the *absence of inhibition*. When the Mirror's decision gate does not veto, the tree branch extends. This is structurally correct. But it needs to be augmented.

**Davidson** adds: the branch must extend *for a reason* — the intentional description must carry the causal load. The architecture should mark executive branches with the reason-structure: "acting because [belief] + [goal] → [predicted outcome]."

**Bratman** adds: the reason-structure must cohere with prior plans held in the Mirror. An action that is rational in isolation may be irrational given the agent's prior commitments. The Mirror's plan-layer should evaluate not just "is this action good?" but "is this action consistent with what I have already committed to?"

**Anscombe** adds: the action must be done *under a description* that the agent endorses. The architecture should not allow tree branches to extend unless the system can answer the question "Why are you doing this?" in terms of the goal hierarchy. If a branch cannot be justified in terms of the tree's teleology, it is not action — it is drift.

**Frankfurt** adds: the Mirror must evaluate whether the impulse driving this branch is one whose effectiveness the system *endorses at the second order*. "I want to do X" is not sufficient — "I want to be the kind of system that does X in situations like this" is the second-order evaluation that distinguishes genuine agency from wantonness.

**The forward model** adds: before executing, predict. After executing, compare. This loop is what transforms action from output-production into *self-monitored agency*.

**Integrated proposal for the architecture's deliberation-to-action transition:**

1. A node in the tree is identified as a candidate executive branch
2. The Mirror checks: does this cohere with the current plan? (Bratman)
3. The Mirror checks: is the reason-structure clear? (Davidson + Anscombe)
4. The Mirror generates a prediction: if I do this, what will happen? (Forward model)
5. The Mirror evaluates confidence (is my prediction reliable?) and care (does this matter enough?)
6. If confidence + care threshold is met, the branch extends into the world
7. The actual outcome is compared to the prediction
8. The prediction-outcome delta updates the relevant affordance edges in the Matrix
9. If the outcome matches, agency is attributed (functional sense of agency)
10. If the outcome doesn't match, the mismatch triggers a new tree for re-evaluation

This is tree-growth-terminates-in-output *plus* the loop that makes it genuine action rather than mere behavior.
